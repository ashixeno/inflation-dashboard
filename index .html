Core Indicator #1
Consumer Price Index
Most widely followed inflation measure. Published monthly by BLS (2nd Tuesday, 8:30 AM ET). Measures price changes for ~80,000 goods and services for urban consumers.
BLS · 2ND TUE 8:30AM ET
Definition & Components
Headline CPI measures all items including food and energy — the number that dominates headlines.

Core CPI excludes food and energy (too volatile) to reveal the underlying structural trend.

Basket: Shelter 36.4% · Services 26.5% · Core Goods 16.2% · Food 13.9% · Energy 7%

Key insight: Shelter's 36% weight makes OER the single most important driver of CPI persistency.
Formula
CPI = (Basket Cost Now / Base Period Cost) × 100
YoY% = (CPI_t / CPI_{t-12} − 1) × 100
MoM% = (CPI_t / CPI_{t-1} − 1) × 100
Core = Headline ex Food (13.9%) & Energy (7%)
Trading impact: 0.1% CPI surprise above consensus moves 10Y yields 5–10bps immediately at 8:30 AM. CPI is the single highest-volatility macro event besides FOMC.

Key Insights — CPI
  • Shelter Lag: OER lags real-time market rents by 12–18 months. This kept CPI artificially high through 2023 even as market rents (Zillow) fell 10%+ from peak.
  • PPI Lead: PPI leads CPI by 3–6 months. PPI hit 11.5% in Nov 2021 — precisely 6 months before CPI peaked at 9.06% in June 2022.
  • 2022 Peak Context: 9.06% was the highest CPI since December 1981. The collapse from 9.06%→3.35% in 18 months was the fastest disinflation since the Volcker era.
  • vs PCE: CPI runs 0.3–0.5% above PCE structurally due to higher shelter weight. The Fed targets PCE (2%), not CPI. At 3.35% CPI, PCE is near 2.6%.
Core Indicator #2 — Fed's Preferred Measure
PCE Price Index
The Federal Reserve's official inflation target. Published by BEA. Uses chained (flexible) weights that adjust for consumer substitution — structurally 0.3–0.5% below CPI.
BEA · MONTHLY · FED TARGET
PCE vs CPI — Critical Differences
Shelter weight: PCE ~24% vs CPI 36.4% → PCE less sensitive to OER lag.

Medical weight: PCE ~16% vs CPI 6.9% → PCE better captures healthcare inflation.

Basket type: PCE uses chained (substitution-adjusted) weights; CPI uses fixed basket.

Coverage: PCE covers all household consumption; CPI only urban consumers (~93% of population).
Why the Fed Prefers PCE
PCE is considered more accurate because it:

(1) Captures substitution — when beef gets expensive, consumers buy chicken. Fixed CPI misses this.

(2) Includes third-party spending — employer-paid health insurance, Medicare/Medicaid services.

(3) Historical revisions — PCE incorporates GDP revisions for comprehensive accuracy.

(4) Less shelter distortion — shelter weight is lower, OER lag has less impact on PCE readings.

Key Insights — PCE & Fed Policy
  • 2% Target Official Since 2012: The Fed formally adopted the 2% PCE target in January 2012. For the subsequent decade, they consistently undershot to the downside.
  • Average Inflation Targeting (AIT): Adopted Aug 2020, allowing temporary overshoot to compensate for below-target years. This delayed tightening and was a critical policy error.
  • 2022 Policy Emergency: Core PCE hit 5.45% in Feb 2022 with the Fed still at 0%. This 345bps gap (actual vs target) required 525bps of emergency tightening over 16 months.
  • Cut Trigger: Markets expect the Fed to cut when Core PCE sustains below 2.5% for 3+ months. At 2.93% (Dec 2023), cuts in H1 2024 became highly probable.
Core Indicator #3 — Pipeline Leading Indicator
Producer Price Index
Measures price changes from the seller's perspective. PPI Final Demand leads CPI by 3–6 months — making it the most critical forward-looking inflation signal available to traders.
BLS · MID-MONTH
The Inflation Pipeline
Stage 1: Raw Materials (crude commodities, farm products)
↓ 1–2 month lag
Stage 2: Intermediate Goods (processed commodities, semi-finished)
↓ 2–3 month lag
Stage 3: PPI Final Demand (finished goods & services)
↓ 3–6 month lag
Stage 4: CPI (consumer prices feel the impact)
PPI Components & Formula
PPI = (Price Level Current / Price Level Base) × 100
Final Demand: Goods 35% + Services 65%
Key relationship: PPI−CPI spread = corporate margin signal
When PPI rises faster than CPI: margin compression → earnings risk → sell sector

When CPI rises faster than PPI: pricing power → margin expansion → buy sector
PPI vs CPI — The Lead Relationship (2004–2023)
PPI Final Demand leads CPI by 3–6 months · Pipeline inflation · Rate comparison
Key Insights — PPI as Leading Indicator
  • The 2021 Warning: PPI surged to 9.6% in Nov 2021 exactly 6 months before CPI peaked at 9.06% in Jun 2022. PPI screamed inflation while the Fed called it transitory.
  • Disinflation Confirmation: PPI turned near-zero in late 2022, confirming the disinflationary trend months before CPI fell. By Dec 2023, PPI was ~1% while CPI was 3.35%.
  • Margin Watch: In 2021–22, PPI (11.5%) outpaced CPI (9.06%) → companies absorbed costs → S&P 500 margins compressed 150bps in 2022. Watch the spread.
  • Trading Signal: PPI beat → short industrial bonds, long defensive sectors, sell growth. PPI miss → long bonds, long discretionary, commodities neutral.
Trade Inflation
Import & Export Price Indices
Import prices directly feed domestic inflation with a 3–6 month lag. The USD is the dominant driver — a 10% DXY appreciation typically reduces import prices by 5–6% within 6–12 months.
BLS · MONTHLY
Import Price Index
Measures price of goods & services purchased by US residents from foreign producers.

Key drivers: USD strength (inverse relationship), global commodity prices, Chinese manufacturing costs, global shipping (Baltic Dry Index).

USD effect: 10% USD appreciation → −5 to −6% import prices within 6–12 months. This is why DXY strength in 2022 partially offset the energy inflation shock on imported goods.
Export Price Index & Trade Dynamics
Measures prices received by US producers selling to foreign buyers.

Key drivers: USD (strong USD makes US exports expensive), global demand (China buying cycles), agricultural weather events.

Tariff risk: New trade tariffs act as a supply-side price shock → raise import prices even when USD is strong. 2018–2019 tariffs added ~0.3% to CPI. 2025+ tariff risks are significant.

Broadest Economy-Wide Measure
GDP Price Deflator
The most comprehensive inflation measure covering the entire economy — consumer spending, business investment, government expenditures, and exports. Updated quarterly with GDP releases.
BEA · QUARTERLY
Definition & Formula
The GDP Deflator converts nominal GDP into real GDP by removing price change effects.
GDP Deflator = (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) × 100
Real GDP = Nominal GDP / (Deflator / 100)
Inflation = (Deflator_t / Deflator_{t-1} − 1) × 100
Unlike CPI, not based on a fixed basket — adjusts for actual economic composition each quarter.
GDP Deflator vs CPI — Why They Differ
Coverage: GDP Deflator covers ALL domestic production (C+I+G+NX). CPI covers only consumer purchases.

Imports: GDP Deflator excludes imports (not domestically produced). CPI includes them — critical difference during energy shocks.

Investment: Deflator includes capital goods (computers, machinery). CPI does not.

Government: Deflator includes government consumption. CPI excludes most government services.

Services Inflation Driver
Wage Inflation — Average Hourly Earnings
Wages are the ultimate source of sustainable inflation. Labour costs represent 60–70% of service business costs — making wage growth the most critical factor for Supercore CPI persistence.
BLS · FIRST FRIDAY 8:30AM
The Wage-Price Spiral
Mechanism: Workers demand higher wages → companies raise prices to cover costs → workers demand more → prices rise further. This self-reinforcing cycle drove 1970s stagflation.

Fed's wage watch:
• Sustainable: ~3–3.5% (2% inflation + 1.5% productivity)
• Warning zone: AHE above 4.5% → services sticky above 3%
• Risk zone: AHE above 5.5% → wage-price spiral risk
Wages & Supercore CPI
Supercore CPI = Services ex-Shelter (~26.5% of CPI). This is entirely labour-cost driven.

At current AHE of 4.1% (Dec 2023), Supercore CPI runs near 4% — still well above target.

Rule of thumb: Supercore lags wage growth by ~6 months. To get Supercore below 3%, AHE must fall to ~3.5% for 6+ months.

Quit Rate signal: JOLTS quit rate leads wage growth by ~3 months. As quits normalized to 2.3%, AHE decelerated from 5.5% → 4.1%.

Shelter Inflation — Housing & Rent
The primary reason CPI remained elevated through 2023 despite falling energy and goods prices. OER (Owner's Equivalent Rent) lags real-time market rents by 12–18 months — creating artificial inflation persistence.
36% OF CPI BASKET
The OER Measurement Problem
Owner's Equivalent Rent (~26% of CPI): Asks homeowners "how much to rent your home?" Survey-based estimate, not market transactions. Updated quarterly from sampled leases.

Rent of Primary Residence (~7%): Actual rent paid by tenants, surveyed quarterly.

The 18-month lag: OER reflects existing lease contracts, not new listings. Zillow peaked Feb 2022; CPI shelter peaked Apr 2023 — 14-month lag. By Dec 2023, market rents were flat/declining while CPI shelter remained at 6.2%.
The Shelter-Rate Paradox
Rate hikes → housing affordability collapses → new supply constrained (developers can't underwrite) → rental demand rises → rents rise → shelter CPI stays elevated → inflation appears stuck despite tight policy.

This creates a policy trap: higher rates cause more shelter inflation in the short term.

Resolution: Real-time rents (Zillow, Apartment List) are falling since mid-2022. OER should normalize to 3–4% by mid-2024, bringing Core CPI to ~2.8%.

Most Volatile Component
Energy Inflation
Most volatile CPI component — ranging from −29% to +41% over 20 years. Oil prices are the primary driver with direct and indirect pass-through to all sectors of the economy.
7% DIRECT WEIGHT · LARGE INDIRECT
Energy CPI Sub-Components
Gasoline (3.6% of CPI): Most immediate WTI/Brent pass-through. Consumers notice at the pump — direct sentiment impact on consumer spending and confidence.

Electricity (2.6%): Driven by natural gas (power generation). Less volatile but stickier than gasoline. Regulatory and infrastructure costs add persistence.

Piped Natural Gas (0.8%): Seasonal — winter heating demand. Major swing in Jan–Feb CPI prints.
Second-Round Effects — Why Energy Matters More Than 7%
Energy is an input cost for virtually everything:

→ Petroleum-based chemicals, plastics, packaging (all goods)
→ Transportation fuel → shipping → all goods prices
→ Manufacturing electricity → industrial costs
→ Agricultural fuel → food production

Empirical estimate: A sustained $20/bbl oil price increase adds ~0.5% to headline CPI within 3 months and 0.2–0.3% to Core CPI within 6–12 months.

Key Insights — Food Inflation
  • 2022 Food Crisis: Ukraine-Russia war disrupted wheat, sunflower oil, and fertilizer markets. FAO Global Food Price Index hit all-time high March 2022. US Food at Home CPI peaked at 13.5% (Aug 2022) — highest since 1979.
  • Restaurant Stickiness: Food Away from Home peaked later and fell slower. Restaurant labour (30%+ of costs) remained elevated, keeping menu prices high even as grocery prices fell sharply.
  • Farm-to-Shelf Lag: Agricultural commodity prices lead grocery prices by 3–6 months. Corn/soybean fell 30% in late 2022; grocery prices took until mid-2023 to ease meaningfully.
  • Political Sensitivity: Food inflation has highest political salience. The 2008 food spike contributed to geopolitical instability globally. In the US, it's a key measure of "living standards" inflation.
Atlanta Fed Methodology
Sticky vs Flexible Inflation
The Atlanta Fed decomposes CPI into sticky prices (infrequent change) and flexible prices (frequent change). This reveals which inflation is structural and which will self-correct — the Fed's most important diagnostic tool.
ATLANTA FED METHODOLOGY
Sticky Price CPI (~70% of basket)
Definition: Items changing less than once per 4.3 months on average. High menu costs. Reflect deeply embedded price expectations.

Examples: Medical services, education, insurance, rent/OER, household services, restaurant meals.

Why it matters: Sticky inflation is the Fed's hardest challenge. Once embedded in business pricing models and wage contracts, it requires sustained restrictive policy to unwind. If sticky inflation rises, the Fed has no easy exit.
Flexible Price CPI (~30% of basket)
Definition: Items changing more often than once per 4.3 months. Respond rapidly to market forces and supply/demand shocks.

Examples: Gasoline, food at home, used cars, airfares, hotel rooms, apparel, commodities.

Transitory vs Structural signal: When flexible CPI surges but sticky stays low → likely transitory. When sticky CPI follows flexible upward → structural inflation requiring aggressive policy response. 2021 was initially just flexible; 2022 became structural as sticky followed.
Sticky vs Flexible CPI + Trimmed Mean PCE (2004–2023)
Persistence of inflation · Atlanta Fed methodology · Transitory vs structural signal
Key Insights — Sticky vs Flexible Dynamics
  • 2021 Sequence: Flexible CPI spiked to 20%+ first (energy, used cars, food). The Fed called it "transitory." Sticky CPI then followed — reaching 8.5% by 2022. Once sticky moved, the "transitory" narrative collapsed.
  • Trimmed Mean PCE: The Dallas Fed trims top/bottom 8% of price changes monthly. This gave the cleanest signal — rising steadily from 1.7% (2020) to 5.9% (2022) with no transitory spike. Markets that tracked this were not surprised by 9% CPI.
  • Current (Dec 2023): Flexible CPI collapsed to near zero (energy deflation). Sticky remains at 5.5% — exactly why the Fed hasn't declared victory. Services, shelter, and wages must ease further.
  • Policy implication: The Fed only needs to watch sticky inflation. If sticky stays below 4%, flexible can spike (oil shock) without requiring emergency hikes — the Fed can "look through" it.
Fisher Equation
Real Interest Rates
The real interest rate — nominal rate minus inflation — is the most important variable in all of finance. It determines the true cost of capital, currency strength, gold prices, and equity valuations.
FISHER EQUATION · KEY DRIVER
The Fisher Equation
Approximate: Real Rate = Nominal Rate − Inflation
Exact (Fisher): (1+i) = (1+r)(1+π)
→ r = (1+i)/(1+π) − 1
Dec 2023: r = 5.33% − 3.35% = +1.98% (restrictive)
2021 peak easy: 0.09% − 7.04% = −6.95% (max stimulus)
Positive real rate = borrowing genuinely costly = restrictive monetary policy.
Negative real rate = easy money = stimulative (the 2020–2022 problem that caused 9% CPI).
Real Rates & Asset Classes
Gold: Inverse to real rates. Surged when real rates went to −6.95% (2021). As real rates rose, gold faced headwinds but central bank buying (China, Russia, EM) provided a structural floor above $1,800.

Equities: High real rates compress P/E multiples. The discount rate for future earnings rises → growth stocks hit hardest (longest duration cashflows). Nasdaq fell 33% in 2022 as real rates rose 500bps.

USD: High real rates attract capital flows → DXY strengthens. In 2022, DXY rose 20% as the US had the highest real rates among G10 currencies.

Why Supercore Matters for Traders
  • Supercore > 4% = Fed will NOT cut. Powell explicitly said this metric needs to fall before they ease. Current 4.1% is the reason Apr 2026 is a HOLD.
  • Supercore ≈ Wages − 0.5%. This rule of thumb holds historically. With wages at 3.8%, Supercore at 4.1% suggests wages need to slow more.
  • CPI surprise = Supercore surprise. When Supercore comes in hot on CPI release day, rates spike and equities sell off. This is the #1 market-moving subcomponent.

Long-Run Perspective
Historical Context — 1960s to 2026
Understanding current inflation requires historical context. The 1970s Great Inflation, Volcker shock, Great Moderation, COVID surge, and current disinflation — every episode has lessons for today.
BLS · Federal Reserve History

US CPI Inflation — Long History (1960–2026)

All major inflation episodes annotated · Recessions shaded · Fed regime changes marked

BLS CPI-U

Every inflation surge has been unique: 1970s = oil shocks + loose Fed; 2021–22 = supply chains + fiscal stimulus + loose Fed. Current episode most similar to 1946–48 (post-war supply shock) rather than 1970s (structural).

Great Inflation (1965–1982) vs COVID Surge (2020–2026)

Overlay comparison — normalized to inflation cycle start

COVID surge reached similar peak speed but shorter duration than 1970s. Volcker hiked to 20% to break 1970s inflation. 2022 episode required only 5.5%.

Volcker Disinflation vs Current Disinflation

Rate of disinflation: 1980–1983 vs 2022–2026

Volcker: 14.8% → 3.2% in 36 months (−11.6pp). Current: 9.1% → 2.4% in 30 months (−6.7pp). Current disinflation comparable speed to Volcker without causing a deep recession.

Key Historical Lessons for Current Episode
  • 1946–1948 analogy: Post-war supply shock inflation resolved when supply chains normalized — without Volcker-style recession. Current episode most closely matches this.
  • 1970s mistake to avoid: Fed cut rates too early in 1975 and 1980, causing inflation to re-accelerate. Fed is explicitly aware of this — hence the pause.
  • Expectations anchoring is everything: In 1970s, inflation expectations de-anchored (workers demanded 10%+ wages). In 2022, they stayed anchored at ~3% — allowing faster disinflation.
  • Last mile is hardest: Getting from 9% → 4% was "easy." Getting from 4% → 2% is proving difficult due to shelter and Supercore stickiness.

How Inflation Swaps Work — Trader Insight
  • Zero Coupon Inflation Swap (ZCIS): Party A pays fixed rate X%. Party B pays realized CPI. If CPI > X%, Party B loses. Pension funds use this to hedge inflation liabilities.
  • Swap > TIPS breakeven = liquidity premium. Current +12bps means TIPS slightly cheap vs swaps.
  • Trading signal: When 1Y swap moves >30bps in a week, a CPI surprise is usually priced in. Apr 2026: 1Y swap at 3.42% vs Jan 2026 at 2.85% = market priced in energy shock ahead of Mar CPI.

Taylor Rule Calculator — What Should the Fed Rate Be?
The Taylor Rule (1993) gives the "correct" policy rate based on inflation gap and output gap. Comparing actual Fed rate vs Taylor Rule reveals whether Fed is hawkish, dovish, or neutral relative to its own framework — the most used Fed analysis tool in macro.
Taylor (1993) · Bloomberg TAYLORR
Taylor Rule Formula — John Taylor (1993)
r* = π + r̄ + α(π − π*) + β(y − ȳ)
π = Current PCE inflation = 2.65%  |  π* = Target = 2.0%  |  r̄ = R* = 0.75%
α = 0.5 (inflation weight)  |  β = 0.5 (output gap weight)  |  y − ȳ ≈ −0.3% (output gap)
